Correctional Informatics

Jails are local correctional institutions that are on the front-line of healthcare in the criminal justice system. They process 13 million admissions a year. Jail inmates are disproportionately male, people of color, and poor.

Public safety stakeholders are not unaware of this trend. Sheriffs and wardens throughout the country frequently comment about how their institutions have become de facto mental health institutions and how jails are poorly equipped to serve in that role.

The Unseen Provider is a short documentary that discusses the dire need for improvement and technological advancement in correctional health.

Since its beginning Community Oriented Correctional Health Services (COCHS) has worked to bridge the gap between correctional and community providers. COCHS' major emphasis has been to re-frame jail healthcare not as a place separate from the rest of the community but as another healthcare delivery site within the community

74% of incarcerated adults spent time in foster care, while 80% of death row inmates were once in state foster care

In collaboration with other organizations, the American Medical Association established the National Comission on Correctional Health Care after a study of jails found inadequate, disorganized health services

Children and young adolescents who come in contact with the juvenile justice system need access to developmentally appropriate, trauma-informed, supportive health and social services

Over 50% of prisoners are diagnosed with a mental health concern or substance abuse disorder and 75% have both

The model for COCHS work originated in Hampden County, Massachusetts. With the understanding that inmates were temporarily displaced community members and continuity of care was important for their successful re-entry
